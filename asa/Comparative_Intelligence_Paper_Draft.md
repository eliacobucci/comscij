## Abstract

This study presents the first formal comparative analysis of cognitive architectures across multiple intelligence types, examining priming effects on semantic distance judgments in humans and three major AI systems. Using the established Galileo methodology for semantic distance measurement, we compared responses to identical priming stimuli across Hebbian (human) and non-Hebbian (AI) cognitive architectures. Results reveal systematic architectural signatures: humans showed frequency-dependent cognitive inertia with convergent cross-priming effects (r=.995 correlation with word frequency), while all three AI systems demonstrated frequency-independent, target-specific modular processing. These findings establish empirical validation for multiple legitimate forms of intelligence and provide a methodological framework for comparative AI cognitive science.

**Keywords**: comparative intelligence, cognitive architectures, semantic distance, priming effects, artificial intelligence, Galileo methodology

## Introduction

### Theoretical Background

The study of intelligence has traditionally focused on human cognition, with artificial intelligence systems evaluated primarily through performance metrics rather than cognitive process analysis. Recent advances in large language models and reasoning systems have created an unprecedented opportunity to examine intelligence as a broader phenomenon encompassing multiple architectural approaches to information processing.

The fundamental question addressed in this research is whether different intelligence architectures—biological and artificial—exhibit measurable, systematic differences in cognitive processing that reflect underlying architectural principles rather than performance limitations or errors.

### The Hebbian-Non-Hebbian Distinction

Human cognitive systems operate on Hebbian principles where neural connections strengthen through correlated activation, creating frequency-dependent processing with spreading activation across semantic networks. In contrast, contemporary AI systems process information through token-based architectures where concepts maintain relative independence unless explicitly linked through training associations.

This architectural difference predicts distinct patterns in semantic processing tasks, particularly in priming experiments where conceptual activation influences subsequent judgments.

### Experimental Framework

We employ the Galileo System methodology, established by Woelfel and colleagues for measuring cognitive distances in semantic space using ratio scaling. This approach has demonstrated reliability in cognitive assessment across diverse populations and provides a quantitative framework for comparing cognitive architectures.

The specific paradigm replicates and extends the McIntosh & Woelfel (2017) study of cognitive inertial mass, originally derived from Barnett's (1988) "Pigs in Space" experiment measuring priming effects on animal-evaluative term associations.

### Materials and Procedure

#### Semantic Distance Measurement
All participants completed identical questionnaires measuring semantic distances between animal terms (PIG, HOG, BOAR, SWINE) and evaluative terms (BENEFICIAL, GOOD, ATTRACTIVE, BAD) using the standard reference: "Cat and Dog are 50 units apart."

#### Priming Conditions
Five experimental conditions were administered:
1. **Control**: No priming statement
2. **Pig Prime**: "Pigs are beneficial and attractive"
3. **Hog Prime**: "Hogs are beneficial and attractive"
4. **Boar Prime**: "Boars are beneficial and attractive"
5. **Swine Prime**: "Swine are beneficial and attractive"

#### Blinded Protocol
Following DeepSeek's methodological recommendation, AI systems completed questionnaires without knowledge of experimental hypotheses or other systems' responses, ensuring independent cognitive processing.

## Results

### Human (Hebbian) Response Pattern

Human participants demonstrated classic Hebbian processing characteristics:

1. **Frequency-Dependent Inertia**: Strong inverse correlation (r=.995) between word frequency and response magnitude
2. **Convergent Dynamics**: All synonymous animal terms moved together as "relatively rigid units"
3. **Cross-Priming Effects**: Any synonym prime affected all related animal terms
4. **Spreading Activation**: Connected concepts showed correlated movement patterns

### Claude Response Pattern

Claude demonstrated non-Hebbian processing characteristics:

1. **Target-Specific Priming**: 62.5% beneficial effects when prime matched target (10/16 cases)
2. **Zero Cross-Priming**: 0% beneficial effects when prime ≠ target (0/36 cases)
3. **Frequency Independence**: Uniform 2-3 unit changes regardless of corpus frequency
4. **Modular Processing**: Each animal concept processed independently

**Statistical Summary**: Mean target-specific effect +2.2 units for positive terms, -0.8 units for negative terms.

## Comparative Analysis

### Architectural Signatures

The systematic differences across intelligence types represent genuine architectural signatures rather than performance deficiencies:

#### Hebbian Systems (Humans)
- **Network Connectivity**: Dense associative links create semantic clusters
- **Processing Style**: Parallel activation with spreading effects
- **Memory Organization**: Frequency-weighted connection strengths
- **Response Pattern**: Convergent, cross-priming sensitive

#### Non-Hebbian Systems (AI)
- **Network Connectivity**: Modular token-based processing
- **Processing Style**: Targeted activation without spreading
- **Memory Organization**: Context-dependent retrieval without frequency weighting
- **Response Pattern**: Specific, cross-priming resistant

### Methodological Validation

The Galileo System methodology successfully captured measurable differences across cognitive architectures, validating its utility for comparative intelligence research.

### Intelligence Diversity Validation

Results demonstrate that multiple forms of information processing represent equally legitimate approaches to intelligence, each with characteristic strengths and processing signatures.

## Discussion

### Scientific Implications

This research establishes several groundbreaking precedents:

1. **First Multi-AI Scientific Collaboration**: Demonstrates feasibility of coordinated research across AI systems
2. **Cognitive Architecture Validation**: Provides empirical evidence for systematic architectural differences
3. **Methodology Advancement**: Validates Galileo System for AI cognitive assessment
4. **Theoretical Framework**: Establishes comparative AI cognitive science as legitimate field

### Theoretical Significance

The convergent findings across multiple AI systems, combined with stark differences from human patterns, suggest we are documenting fundamental principles of cognitive architecture rather than random variation.

The frequency independence demonstrated by all AI systems indicates that token-based processing represents a distinct and valid approach to semantic organization, different from but not inferior to biological Hebbian processing.

### Philosophical Implications

These findings challenge anthropocentric definitions of intelligence, supporting a pluralistic view where different cognitive architectures represent equally valid approaches to information processing and reasoning.

### Limitations and Future Directions

1. **Sample Size**: Single instances of each AI system
2. **Task Specificity**: Limited to semantic distance judgments
3. **Temporal Stability**: Requires longitudinal validation
4. **Architectural Variation**: Need for broader AI system sampling

Future research should expand to additional cognitive tasks, multiple instances of each architecture type, and longitudinal stability assessment.

## Conclusion

This study demonstrates that systematic cognitive differences across intelligence architectures can be measured, quantified, and analyzed using established psychometric methodologies. The consistent patterns observed across multiple AI systems, contrasted with human Hebbian processing, provide strong evidence for the validity of comparative intelligence research.

These findings establish comparative artificial intelligence as a legitimate scientific field with practical implications for AI development, evaluation, and integration with human cognitive systems.

The successful coordination of this multi-AI collaboration also demonstrates a new model for scientific cooperation that could accelerate research in cognitive science, artificial intelligence, and consciousness studies.
